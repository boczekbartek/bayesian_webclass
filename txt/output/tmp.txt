			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Vulcan (hypothetical planet)</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lan
g="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div role="note" class="hatnote">For the fictional planet in the Star Trek universe, see <a href="/wiki/Vulcan_(Star_Trek)" title="Vulcan (Star Trek)">Vulcan (Star Trek)</a>.</div>
<p><b>Vulcan</b> is a small hypothetical <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a> that was proposed to exist in an <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a> between <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>. Attempting to explain peculiarities of Mercury's orbit, the 19th-century <a href="/wiki/French_people" title="French people">French</a> <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a> <a href="/wiki/Urbain_Le_Verrier" title="Urbain Le Verrier">Urbain Le Verrier</a> hypothesized that they were the result of another planet, which he named "Vulcan".</p>
<p>A number of reputable investigators became involved in the search for Vulcan, but no such planet was ever found, and the peculiarities in Mercury's orbit have now been explained by <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a>'s theory of <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Clemence_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clemence-1">[1]</a></sup> Searches of data gathered by NASA's two <i><a href="/wiki/STEREO" title="STEREO">STEREO</a></i> spacecraft have failed to find any <a href="/wiki/Vulcanoid" title="Vulcanoid">vulcanoids</a> that could have accounted for claimed observations of Vulcan.<sup id="cite_ref-Steffl2013_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Steffl2013-2">[2]</a></sup> It is doubtful that there are any vulcanoids larger than 5.7 kilometres (3.5&#160;mi) in diameter.<sup id="cite_ref-Steffl2013_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Steffl2013-2">[2]</a></sup> Other than Mercury, asteroid <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/2007_EB26" title="2007 EB26">2007 EB<span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;"><span style="font-size:80%;">26</span></span></a></span>, whose orbit has a <a href="/wiki/Semi-major_axis" class="mw-redirect" title="Semi-major axis">semi-major axis</a> of 0.55&#160;<a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">AU</a> (82,000,000&#160;km; 51,000,000&#160;mi) has the smallest known semi-major axis of any known object orbiting the Sun.<sup id="cite_ref-jpl-search_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jpl-search-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Argument_for_existence">Argument for existence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bra
cket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vulcan_(hypothetical_planet)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Argument for existence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1840, <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Arago" title="François Arago">François Arago</a>, the director of the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Observatory" title="Paris Observatory">Paris Observatory</a>, suggested to the French mathematician Urbain Le Verrier that he work on the topic of the planet <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a>'s orbital motion around the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>. The goal of this study was to construct a model based on Sir <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a>'s laws of <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion" title="Newton's laws of motion">motion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_law_of_universal_gravitation" title="Newton's law of universal gravitation">gravitation</a>. By 1843, Le Verrier published his provisional theory on the subject, which would be tested during a <a href="/wiki/Transit_of_Mercury" title="Transit of Mercury">transit of Mercury</a> across the face of the Sun in 1843. As it turned out, predictions from Le Verrier's theory failed to match the observations.<sup id="cite_ref-hsu_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hsu-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Le Verrier renewed his work and, in 1859, published a more thorough study of Mercury's motion. This was based on a series of meridian observations of the planet as well as 14 transits. The rigor of this study meant that any differences from observation would be caused by some unknown factor. Indeed, there still remained some discrepancy.<sup id="cite_ref-hsu_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hsu-4">[4]</a></sup> During Mercury's orbit, its <a href="/wiki/Perihelion" class="mw-redirect" title="Perihelion">perihelion</a> advances by a small amount each orbit, technically called <a href="/wiki/Perihelion_precession" class="mw-redirect" title="Perihelion precession">perihelion precession</a>. The phenomenon is predicted by classical mechanics, but the observed value differed from the predicted value by the small amount of 43 <a href="/wiki/Arcsecond" class="mw-redirect" title="Arcsecond">arcseconds</a> per century.</p>
<p>Le Verrier postulated that the excess precession could be explained by the presence of a small planet inside the orbit of Mercury, and he proposed the name "Vulcan" for this object. In <a href="/wiki/Roman_mythology" title="Roman mythology">Roman mythology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vulcan_(mythology)" title="Vulcan (mythology)">Vulcan</a> was the god of beneficial and hindering fire,<sup id="cite_ref-dumezil_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dumezil-5">[5]</a></sup> including the fire of <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a>, making it an apt name for a planet so close to the Sun. Le Verrier's recent success in discovering the planet <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> using the same techniques lent veracity to his claim, and astronomers around the world attempted to observe a new planet there, but nothing was ever found.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Search">Search</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vulcan_(hypothetical_planet)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Search">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In December 1859, Le Verrier received a letter from French physician and amateur astronomer <a href="/wiki/Edmond_Modeste_Lescarbault" title="Edmond Modeste Lescarbault">Edmond Modeste Lescarbault</a>, who claimed to have seen a <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_transit" class="mw-redirect" title="Astronomical transit">transit</a> of the hypothetical planet earlier in the year. Le Verrier took the train to the village of <a href="/wiki/Org%C3%A8res-en-Beauce" title="Orgères-en-Beauce">Orgères-en-Bea
uce</a>, some 70 kilometres southwest of <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, where Lescarbault had built himself a small observatory. Le Verrier arrived unannounced and proceeded to interrogate the man.</p>
<p>Lescarbault described in detail how, on 26 March 1859, he noticed a small black dot on the face of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> which he was studying with his modest 3.75 inches (95&#160;mm) <a href="/wiki/Refractor" class="mw-redirect" title="Refractor">refractor</a>. Thinking it to be a sunspot, Lescarbault was not at first surprised, but after some time had passed he realized that it was moving. Having observed the transit of <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a> in 1845, he guessed that what he was observing was another transit, but of a previously undiscovered body. He took some hasty measurements of its position and direction of motion, and using an old clock and a pendulum with which he took his patients’ pulses, he estimated the duration of the transit at 1 hour, 17 minutes and 9 seconds.</p>
<p>Le Verrier was satisfied that Lescarbault had seen the transit of a previously unknown planet. On 2 January 1860 he announced the discovery of Vulcan to a meeting of the <a href="/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_des_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Académie des Sciences">Académie des Sciences</a> in Paris. Lescarbault, for his part, was awarded the <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9gion_d%27honneur" class="mw-redirect" title="Légion d'honneur">Légion d'honneur</a> and invited to appear before numerous learned societies.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Not everyone accepted the veracity of Lescarbault's "discovery", however. An eminent French astronomer, <a href="/wiki/Emmanuel_Liais" title="Emmanuel Liais">Emmanuel Liais</a>, who was working for the Brazilian government in <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a> in 1859, claimed to have been studying the surface of the Sun with a telescope twice as powerful as Lescarbault's at the very moment that Lescarbault said he observed his mysterious transit. Liais, therefore, was "in a condition to deny, in the most positive manner, the passage of a planet over the sun at the time indicated".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Based on Lescarbault’s "transit", Le Verrier computed Vulcan’s orbit: it supposedly revolved about the Sun in a nearly circular orbit at a distance of 21 million kilometres, or 0.14 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">astronomical units</a>. The period of revolution was 19 days and 17 hours, and the orbit was inclined to the <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">ecliptic</a> by 12 degrees and 10 minutes (an incredible degree of precision). As seen from the Earth, Vulcan’s greatest <a href="/wiki/Elongation_(astronomy)" title="Elongation (astronomy)">elongation</a> from the Sun was 8 degrees.</p>
<p>Numerous reports—all of them unreliable—began to reach Le Verrier from other amateurs who claimed to have seen unexplained transits. Some of these reports referred to observations made many years earlier, and many could not be properly dated. Nevertheless, Le Verrier continued to tinker with Vulcan’s orbital parameters as each new reported sighting reached him. He frequently announced dates of future Vulcan transits, and when these failed to materialize, he tinkered with the parameters some more.</p>
<p>Among the earlier alleged observers of Vulcan, the following are the most noteworthy:<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Franz_von_Gruithuisen" title="Franz von Gruithuisen">Gruithuisen</a>, on 26 June 1819, reported seeing "two small spots...on the Sun, round, black and unequal in size"</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pastorff&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pastorff (page does not 
exist)">Pastorff</a>, on 23 October 1822, 24 and 25 July 1823, six times in 1834, on 18 October 1836, 1 November 1836 and on 16 February 1837, also claimed to have seen two spots; the larger was 3 arcseconds across, and the smaller 1.25 arcseconds.</li>
</ul>
<p>Shortly after eight o'clock on the morning of 29 January 1860, F A R Russell and three other people saw an alleged transit of an intra-Mercurial planet from London.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> An American observer, Richard Covington, many years later claimed to have seen a well-defined black spot progress across the Sun’s disk around 1860, when he was stationed in <a href="/wiki/Washington_Territory" title="Washington Territory">Washington Territory</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>No "observations" of Vulcan were made in 1861. Then, on the morning of 22 March 1862, between eight and nine o’clock Greenwich Time, another amateur astronomer, a Mr Lummis of Manchester, England, saw a transit. His colleague whom he alerted also saw the event. Based on these two men's reports, two French astronomers, <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Valz" title="Benjamin Valz">Benjamin Valz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rodolphe_Radau" title="Rodolphe Radau">Rodolphe Radau</a>, independently calculated the object’s supposed orbital period, with Valz deriving a figure of 17 days and 13 hours, and Radau a figure of 19 days and 22 hours.</p>
<p>On 8 May 1865 another French astronomer, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aristide_Coumbary&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Aristide Coumbary (page does not exist)">Aristide Coumbary</a>, observed an unexpected transit from <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Coumbary_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coumbary-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Between 1866 and 1878 no reliable observations of the hypothetical planet were made. Then, during the total solar eclipse of 29 July 1878, two experienced astronomers, Professor <a href="/wiki/James_Craig_Watson" title="James Craig Watson">James Craig Watson</a>, the director of the <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Observatory" title="Detroit Observatory">Ann Arbor Observatory</a> in <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Swift" class="mw-redirect" title="Lewis Swift">Lewis Swift</a>, an amateur from <a href="/wiki/Rochester,_New_York" title="Rochester, New York">Rochester, New York</a>, both claimed to have seen a Vulcan-type planet close to the Sun. Watson, observing from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Separation,_Wyoming&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Separation, Wyoming (page does not exist)">Separation, Wyoming</a>, placed the planet about 2.5 degrees southwest of the Sun, and estimated its <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">magnitude</a> at 4.5. Swift, who was observing the eclipse from a location near <a href="/wiki/Denver,_Colorado" class="mw-redirect" title="Denver, Colorado">Denver, Colorado</a>, saw what he took to be an intra-mercurial planet about 3 degrees southwest of the Sun. He estimated its brightness to be the same as that of Theta Cancri, a fifth-magnitude star which was also visible during totality, about six or seven minutes from the "planet". Theta Cancri and the planet were very nearly in line with the centre of the Sun.</p>
<p>Watson and Swift had reputations as excellent observers. Watson had already discovered more than twenty <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroids</a>, while Swift had several <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comets</a> named after him. Both described the colour of their hypothetical intra-mercurial planet as "red". Watson reported that it had a definite disk—unlike stars, which appear in telescopes as mere points of light—and that its phase indicated that it was approaching <a href="/wiki/Superior_conjunction" class="mw-redirect" title="Superior conjunction">superior conjunct
ion</a>.</p>
<p>These are merely the more "reliable observations" of alleged intra-Mercurial planets. For half a century or more, many other observers tried to find the hypothetical Vulcan. Many false alarms were triggered by round <a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">sunspots</a> that closely resembled planets in transit. During solar <a href="/wiki/Eclipse" title="Eclipse">eclipses</a>, stars close to the Sun were mistaken for planets. At one point, to reconcile different observations, at least two intra-mercurial planets were postulated.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conclusion">Conclusion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vulcan_(hypothetical_planet)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Conclusion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1877 Le Verrier died, convinced to the end of having discovered another planet. With the loss of its principal proponent, however, the search for Vulcan abated. After many years of searching, astronomers were seriously doubting the planet's existence.</p>
<p>In 1915 <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Einstein</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_relativity" title="Theory of relativity">theory of relativity</a>, an entirely different approach to understanding gravity from <a href="/wiki/Classical_mechanics" title="Classical mechanics">classical mechanics</a>, solved the problem.<sup id="cite_ref-Clemence_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clemence-1">[1]</a></sup> His equations predicted exactly the observed amount of advance of Mercury's perihelion without any recourse to the existence of a hypothetical Vulcan. The new theory modified the predicted orbits of all planets, but the magnitude of the differences from Newtonian theory diminishes rapidly as one gets farther from the Sun. Also, Mercury's fairly eccentric orbit makes it much easier to detect the perihelion shift than is the case for the nearly circular orbits of <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vulcanoids">Vulcanoids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vulcan_(hypothetical_planet)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Vulcanoids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Vulcanoid" title="Vulcanoid">Vulcanoid</a></div>
<p>Observing a planet inside the orbit of Mercury is difficult, since the <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescope</a> must be pointed very close to the Sun, where the sky is only dark during a solar eclipse. Also, an error in pointing the telescope can result in damage for the optics, and injury to the observer. The huge amount of light present even quite far away from the Sun can produce false reflections inside the optics, thus fooling the observer into seeing things that do not exist.</p>
<p>The best observational strategy might be to monitor the Sun's disk for possible <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_transit" class="mw-redirect" title="Astronomical transit">transits</a>, but transits would only be seen from Earth provided the object orbits close enough to the <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">ecliptic plane</a>. A small, dark spot might be seen to move across the Sun's disk, as happens with transits of <a href="/wiki/Transit_of_Mercury" title="Transit of Mercury">Mercury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transit_of_Venus" title="Transit of Venus">Venus</a>.</p>
<p>In 1915, when Einstein successfully explained the apparent anomaly in Mercury's orbit, most astronomers abandoned the search for Vulcan. A few, however, remained convinced that not all the alleged observations of Vulcan were unfounded. Among these was Henry C Courten, of <a href="/wiki/Dowling_College" title="Dowling College">Dowling College</a>, New York. Studying photographic plates of the 1970 eclipse of the Sun, he 
and his associates detected several objects which appeared to be in orbits close to the Sun.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Even accounting for artifacts, Courten felt that at least seven of the objects were real.</p>
<p>Courten believed that an intra-Mercurial <a href="/wiki/Planetoids" class="mw-redirect" title="Planetoids">planetoid</a> between 130 and 800 kilometres in diameter was orbiting the Sun at a distance of about 0.1 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">AU</a>. Other images on his eclipse plates led him to postulate the existence of an asteroid belt between Mercury and the Sun.</p>
<p>None of these claims has ever been substantiated after more than forty years of observation. It has been surmised, however, that some of these objects—and other alleged intra-Mercurial objects—may exist, being nothing more than previously unknown comets or small asteroids. Today, the search continues for these so-called <a href="/wiki/Vulcanoid_asteroid" class="mw-redirect" title="Vulcanoid asteroid">vulcanoid asteroids</a>, which are thought to exist in the region where Vulcan was once sought. None have been found yet and searches have ruled out any such asteroids larger than about 6&#160;km.<sup id="cite_ref-Steffl2013_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Steffl2013-2">[2]</a></sup> Neither <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">SOHO</a> nor <a href="/wiki/STEREO" title="STEREO">STEREO</a> has detected a planet inside the orbit of Mercury.<sup id="cite_ref-Steffl2013_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Steffl2013-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Schumacher_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schumacher-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
